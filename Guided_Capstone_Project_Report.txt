Guided Capstone Project Report
1. Executive Summary

Big Mountain Resort currently charges $81 for an adult weekend ticket. Using a predictive model built from data on other ski resorts, we estimate that Big Mountain’s facilities could support a ticket price of approximately $94. This suggests the resort may be underpricing its tickets compared to similar resorts.

In this report, we look at Big Mountain’s current position, compare it to other resorts, and explore four scenarios to see how changes in facilities might affect ticket prices and revenue.

2. Data Overview and Exploratory Analysis

We used a dataset of 277 ski resorts including features such as terrain, lifts, snow coverage, number of runs, and regional market context.

Some key observations:

Vertical Drop: Big Mountain ranks high compared to most resorts, but a few resorts have even higher drops.

Snow-Making Area: Among the largest in the dataset, which helps ensure reliable conditions.

Total Chairs and Fast Quads: Big Mountain has a high number of chairs and fast quads, which is good for reducing wait times.

Runs and Longest Run: Offers a lot of options for visitors, with one of the longer runs in the dataset.

Figures 1–5 show the distributions of these features, with Big Mountain marked on each. These plots help us see where the resort stands relative to competitors.

3. Predictive Modeling

We built a random forest regression model to predict adult weekend ticket price based on resort features. The model was trained on all resorts except Big Mountain to avoid biasing the prediction.

Cross-validated MAE: $10.31 ± $1.29

Predicted Big Mountain price: $94.33

Actual price: $81

This shows that Big Mountain could likely charge more. However, the model does not include operational costs like staffing or snow-making, so the suggested price is based on market comparisons, not profitability.

4. Scenario Analysis

We tested four scenarios to see how changes in facilities would affect ticket price and revenue:

Closing Runs: Closing 1–10 of the least-used runs had little effect at first. Closing 6+ runs leads to noticeable drops in ticket price and revenue.

Recommendation: Close runs gradually, focusing on underused ones.

Add Run + Increase Vertical Drop + New Chair Lift:

Ticket price increase: $2.12

Expected revenue increase: $3.7M

Scenario 2 + 2 Acres Snow Making: No significant change in ticket price.

Increase Longest Run + 4 Acres Snow Making: No effect on ticket price.

Figures 6–7 show predicted ticket price and revenue changes for Scenario 1 and Scenario 2.

5. Recommendations

Price Adjustment: Consider raising weekend ticket price toward the modeled support ($94), monitoring customer response.

Facility Improvements: Adding runs and a new chair lift is worth exploring, as it can increase ticket price and revenue.

Run Closures: Only close underused runs and track impact on revenue.

Revenue Tracking: Make sure that added costs for lifts or snow-making don’t outweigh expected revenue.

Model Tool: Allow business analysts to use the model interactively to explore future scenarios.

6. Further Work

Include more cost information to evaluate profitability.

Gather visitor feedback to check price sensitivity.

Update the model regularly with new data to maintain accuracy.

Use scenario simulations for leadership decisions without requiring data team support every time.

Conclusion: Big Mountain is currently underpriced compared to similar resorts. Small facility improvements and a moderate price increase could boost revenue, while careful planning is needed for run closures.
